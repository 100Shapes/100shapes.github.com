Good Design

Designing is hard. Here's how you can measure the sucess of a design to improve your own products and services. I'm an Interaction designer at 100Shapes and this is my perspective on how I think about design.

By Michele Memoli on 2012-10-30

How do you know when a design's good?

I've asked every User Experience (UX) candidate the same question. Here's some things I've heard people say:

  • "It looks good"
  • "It pushes boundaries and breaks the rules"
  • "It's usable"
  • "It solves a problem"

I don't think many people would disagree with the last one – most people would say that designing is about solving a problem. This means a good design just solves a problem well.

When we talk about good design, we're talking about its ability to solve a problem. They are the same thing.

Now we have something to work with: to solve a problem, we need to understand it. For me, good design starts with the question "What am I trying to solve?". When I can answer that, I have something to measure the success of the design.

What's the problem?

When you understand a problem, it's easier to solve. Understanding it means you can quickly tell if a new suggestion is a better answer. When you have lots of solutions, you can choose the best fit.

The best fit is the best solution. The best solution is the best design.

Sometimes the problem might be simple: "get more downloads" or "make more money".

Within the UX field, we have lots of ways to help understand a problem:

  • Stakeholder Interviews
  • Diary studies
  • Competitor Analysis

Each method tells its own story about the problem. By choosing the right one, you get a better understanding of what you're trying to solve. Read up on these techniques and use them in your design process. If you're not a designer, think about the tools you could use to give you answers to your problem.

When you know what the problem is, and what you want to achieve, you can start to think about how you want to achieve it.
